# Positioning Framework: Why You / Why Them / Why Now

A detailed guide tailored for independent logistics and supply chain consultants.

---

## Why this framework exists

Most logistics consultants position themselves too broadly. "I help companies improve their supply chain" sounds like everyone else. The Why You / Why Them / Why Now framework forces specificity by answering three hard questions:

1. **Why You** — What specific expertise, track record, or point of view do you bring that a competitor cannot easily replicate?
2. **Why Them** — Which specific client, in which vertical, at which stage, with which problem, will pay for your help?
3. **Why Now** — Why is this the right time for them to act? What has changed?

When all three answers align, you have a positioning statement that is credible, memorable, and defensible.

---

## Step 1: Why Them (start here)

**Do not start with yourself.** Starting with Why You leads to generic positioning because you try to appeal to everyone. Start with Why Them — the client — and work backwards.

### Questions to answer

| Question | Why it matters | Example (warehouse tech consultant) |
|---|---|---|
| What industry vertical(s)? | Logistics is not one market. 3PL, retail, manufacturing, and cold chain each have different problems. | Mid-market 3PLs and freight forwarders |
| What company size range? | A 20-person freight broker needs different help than a 500-person 3PL. Be honest about where you add most value. | EUR 50–500M annual revenue |
| Who is the decision-maker? | The person with budget authority. | VP Supply Chain, Head of Logistics |
| What is their specific pain? | Vague pain = vague positioning. Name the operational metric. | Picking error rates above 0.5%, peak-season overtime costs, no real-time visibility for customers |
| What have they already tried? | Shows you understand their journey and why DIY has not worked. | Spreadsheets, quick-fix WMS add-ons, asking the IT generalist to "build something" |
| What is the cost of inaction? | Creates urgency. | Losing RFPs because customers demand real-time tracking; compliance risk under CSRD/DSCSA |

### Common pitfalls

- **Too broad:** "Mid-market logistics companies." Which part of logistics? Which geography? What revenue band?
- **Too narrow:** "3PLs in Luxembourg with exactly 147 employees." You need a viable market.
- **Based on wishful thinking:** "Fortune 500 CPG companies." If you have never worked with them, start with who will actually hire you.

### Exercise

Write one paragraph answering all six questions above. Then cut it to two sentences. That is your Why Them anchor.

## Step 2: Why You

Now that you know the client, describe why you are the right person for them.

### What to include

| Element | What to avoid | What works |
|---|---|---|
| Concrete results | "I have helped many clients improve." | "I have cut picking errors from 1.2% to 0.25% across six implementations." |
| Relevant count | "I have a lot of experience." | "Six end-to-end WCS implementations in three countries." |
| Point of view | "I use industry best practices." | "I believe mid-market 3PLs need lightweight WCS, not tier-1 WMS overhauls." |
| Your limitations | "I can do anything." | "I focus on warehouse operations, not transportation. You need a different consultant for fleet optimisation." |

### Common Pitfalls & Edge Cases

-   **The New Consultant Problem:** What if you don't have six implementations to point to? If you're transitioning from an operator role, your 'Why You' is your direct, recent, hands-on experience.
    *   **Weak:** "I have 15 years of experience at Nokia."
    *   **Strong:** "For 15 years at Nokia, I ran the APAC 3PL network. I was the client. I know why the legacy 3PLs' systems fail because I dealt with the fallout."
-   **Confusing 'You' with your CV:** 'Why You' is not a list of your past jobs. It's the *pattern* of problems you solved across those jobs.
-   **Being a 'strategic partner'**: This is a weak position. It means nothing. Be a specialist who solves a specific, expensive problem.
-   **"I can do anything"**: The quickest way to signal you're a generalist with no deep expertise. Defining your non-service is as important as defining your service. It shows focus.

### Exercise

Write down:
- Three specific metrics you have moved in client engagements.
- The number of times you have delivered the core service.
- One strong point of view that differentiates you from other consultants.
- One thing you deliberately do not do (your non-service).

Combine these into a two-sentence Why You statement.

## Step 3: Why Now

Timing is a positioning asset. If the client could have hired you last year or next year with the same urgency, you do not have a compelling Why Now.

### Sources of timing

| Source | Logistics-specific examples |
|---|---|
| Regulatory change | EU CSRD (inventory accuracy reporting from 2025), US DSCSA (serialisation), UK REACH, carbon reporting mandates |
| Technology shift | AI-driven demand planning becoming accessible to mid-market; WMS vendors ending support for legacy versions |
| Market disruption | Post-pandemic inventory normalisation, nearshoring trends, peak-season capacity crunch |
| Organisational trigger | New VP Supply Chain hired; company acquired and needs integration; private equity-backed 3PL scaling rapidly |
| Talent gap | Harder to find warehouse managers who understand both operations and systems; consultants fill the gap |

### The test

If you remove the Why Now reason, does the pitch still work? If yes, you need a stronger Why Now.

Example with Why Now:
> "Manual processes are no longer viable under CSRD and DSCSA."

Example without:
> "Warehouse accuracy is important." (Has always been important — no urgency.)

### Exercise

List three things that are different this year compared to last year for your target client. Pick the strongest one as your Why Now anchor.

---

## Step 4: Assemble the positioning statement

**Formula:**

> I help [Why Them — specific client with specific pain] [achieve/avoid/build X specific outcome]. [Why You — evidence of having done it before]. [Why Now — the timing that makes this urgent].

**Example:**

> I help mid-market 3PLs and freight forwarders (EUR 50–500M) build warehouse control systems that cut error rates below 0.3% and reduce peak-season overtime by 40%. Six implementations, three countries. The mid-market cannot afford tier-1 integrators, and manual processes are no longer viable under CSRD and DSCSA.

---

## Step 5: Test your positioning

Run your statement through these checks:

| Test | Pass Criteria (Hardened) |
|---|---|
| The Specificity Test | A peer could name 1–2 *actual* competitors. If they say "any of the Big Four could do that," your positioning is too broad. It should feel like a niche. |
| The "So What?" Test | Does it solve an expensive, urgent problem? If the client's reaction is "that's nice to have," it will be impossible to sell. It must connect to revenue, cost, or risk. |
| The Portfolio Test | Your last three relevant projects fit squarely inside this frame. If they don't, your positioning is aspirational, not actual. Be honest about what people pay you for *today*. |
| The Price Anchor Test | Your positioning justifies your day rate. If you sound like a generalist, you will be paid like one. Specialism commands a premium. |
| The Referral Test | A client can describe what you do to a peer in a single, clear sentence. If they stumble, your positioning is too complex. |
