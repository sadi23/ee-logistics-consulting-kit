# Personal Brand

## Overview

The Personal Brand skill helps independent logistics consultants build a coherent, differentiated professional identity. It is built around the **Why You, Why Them, Why Now** framework — a structured way to articulate your unique value, the clients you serve best, and the timing that makes you relevant.

Instead of generic "I help companies with supply chain" positioning, this skill pushes you to specific, defensible ground: the verticals you know, the problems you have actually solved, and the decision-makers who will pay for that expertise.

---

## Capabilities

### A: Brand Foundation — Why You / Why Them / Why Now

Helps you produce a crisp positioning statement by answering three questions:

- **Why You** — Your specific expertise, track record, and point of view. Not your CV, but the pattern of problems you solve best.
- **Why Them** — The ideal client profile: industry vertical, company size, operational context, and the specific pain they feel.
- **Why Now** — The market timing, regulatory shift, or operational gap that makes your offering timely.

**Output:** One-paragraph positioning statement + three supporting rationale cards.

**Quick start — logistics consultant example:**

> "I help mid-market 3PLs and freight forwarders (EUR 50–500M revenue) build warehouse control systems that cut error rates below 0.3% and reduce peak-season overtime by 40%. I have done it across six implementations in three countries. Regulatory pressure on inventory accuracy (EU CSRD, US FDA DSCSA) means manual processes are no longer viable — and the mid-market cannot afford the tier-1 integrators."

Work through each card one at a time. Start with Why Them — it is easier to define the client than yourself. Then Why You (your fit to that client). Then Why Now (the window).

### B: Audience & Channel Map

Identifies who needs to hear your message and where they actually pay attention.

**Process:**
1. Name 3–5 supply chain decision-maker roles (e.g. VP Supply Chain, Head of Logistics, Director of Warehouse Operations, Procurement Lead, Supply Chain IT Manager).
2. For each role, identify 2–3 channels they use for professional information (LinkedIn, industry events, trade publications, podcasts, analyst reports).
3. Map your message format to the channel (short-form post vs. conference talk vs. white paper vs. podcast appearance).
4. Prioritise 2–3 channels to own well rather than spreading across all.

**Output:** A table (or structured list) mapping audience role -> channel -> message format -> publish frequency.

**Quick start — logistics consultant example:**

| Audience role | Channel | Format | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| VP Supply Chain (mid-market 3PL) | LinkedIn | Long-form posts + data visualisations | 2x/week |
| Head of Logistics | Supply Chain Dive / DC Velocity | Guest article or quoted opinion | 1x/month |
| Director of Warehouse Ops | Industry webinars & peer groups | Case-study talk | 1x/quarter |
| Supply Chain IT Manager | Logistics Podcasts | Interview appearance | 1x/quarter |

### C: Content Pillars & Cadence

A sustainable content plan anchored to your positioning. Each pillar is a topic area you can write about repeatedly without running out of material.

**Process:**
1. Derive 3–4 content pillars from your positioning statement. Each pillar is a question or problem your audience cares about.
2. For each pillar, list 5–10 specific angles (headlines or talking points).
3. Set a cadence you can actually keep: one post per week is better than five posts for two weeks followed by silence.

**Output:** Pillar card for each topic + a 4-week rolling content calendar.

**Quick start — logistics consultant example pillars:**
- **Pillar 1 — Warehouse Tech for the Mid-Market:** "You do not need a USD 2M WMS. Here is what works at 50–500M revenue."
- **Pillar 2 — Peak Season Without the Panic:** "How to plan headcount, inventory, and system capacity for Q4 without burning out your team."
- **Pillar 3 — Regulatory Pressure on Inventory:** "CSRD, DSCSA, and the coming accuracy standards — what mid-market logistics leaders need to know."
- **Pillar 4 — The Consultant's Toolkit:** "Frameworks and templates I use in client engagements (free-to-share versions)."

Cadence: 1 pillar per week, rotating. One LinkedIn post per week, one newsletter or article per month, one long-form piece per quarter.

### D: Bio Library

A set of pre-written bios for different contexts. Each bio targets a specific audience and medium. Never write a bio from scratch again.

**Bios included:**
1. **LinkedIn Profile** — Professional summary for the About section. First-person, peer tone, specific results.
2. **Speaker/Conference** — Third-person, credential-forward, designed for event programmes and introductions.
3. **Proposal/Engagement** — First-person credibility section for consulting proposals. Focuses on problem fit and outcomes.
4. **Podcast/Media** — Short, narrative, designed to be read by a host. Emphasises point of view over CV.

See `references/bio-library-templates.md` for full templates and a fill-in-the-blanks workbook.

### E: Brand Refresh

Use this when your existing brand has drifted — you are getting inquiries that do not fit, your LinkedIn feels generic, or you sound the same as five other consultants.

**Approach:**
1. Audit your current positioning by reviewing your last 10 LinkedIn posts, your current bio, and your proposal intro. Highlight everything that is generic.
2. Run the Why You / Why Them / Why Now framework against what you actually do today (not what you used to do).
3. Identify the delta — what you used to say vs. what you should say now.
4. Rewrite your bio, update your LinkedIn headline, and replace your pinned post.
5. Archive old content rather than deleting it (consistency has value even if the positioning has shifted).

**Output:** A before/after comparison table and a 30-day transition plan.

**Quick start — logistics consultant example:**

| Element | Before (drifted) | After (refreshed) |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn headline | Supply Chain Consultant | Mid-Market Warehouse Control Systems for 3PLs |
| Bio opener | I help companies improve their supply chain | I help 50–500M 3PLs cut picking errors below 0.3% |
| Pinned post | General supply chain trends | Case study: how one freight forwarder saved 40% on overtime |
| Proposal intro | I have 15 years of experience | I have implemented WC systems across 6 mid-market 3PLs in 3 countries |

## Worked example — full positioning statement

For a logistics consultant specialising in warehouse control systems for mid-market 3PLs:

**Why Them:**
> Mid-market 3PLs and freight forwarders with EUR 50–500M in annual revenue. They run 1–5 warehouses, use a mix of WMS, ERP, and spreadsheets, and are starting to feel the pressure of customer demands for real-time visibility. They have outgrown the "good enough" setup but cannot justify the time and cost a big integrator would demand.

**Why You:**
> Six end-to-end WCS implementations across three countries. I know the mid-market stack — what works with a limited IT team, how to integrate with the WMS you already have, and what to defer for phase two. I have cut picking errors from 1.2% to 0.25% on average and reduced peak-season overtime by 40% across those engagements.

**Why Now:**
> Three converging pressures: (1) EU CSRD requires inventory accuracy reporting from 2025. (2) US DSCSA serialisation deadlines are forcing system upgrades anyway. (3) The talent shortage means you cannot paper-over process gaps with cheap labour any more. Waiting costs more than acting.

**Positioning statement:**
> I help mid-market 3PLs and freight forwarders (EUR 50–500M) build warehouse control systems that cut error rates below 0.3% and reduce peak-season overtime by 40%. Six implementations, three countries. The mid-market cannot afford tier-1 integrators, and manual processes are no longer viable under CSRD and DSCSA.

## Workflow & Dependencies

The capabilities are designed to be run in sequence, especially for a new consultant. **Capability A (Brand Foundation) is the starting point.** The positioning statement it produces is a required input for all other capabilities. Do not skip it.

1.  **Start with A: Brand Foundation.** Get the positioning statement right first.
2.  **Move to B: Audience & Channel Map.** Use the positioning statement to identify the right audience.
3.  **Then C: Content Pillars.** Use the positioning and audience map to build a relevant content plan.
4.  **Finally D: Bio Library.** Use the core positioning to write consistent, powerful bios.
5.  **Use E: Brand Refresh** as needed (every 6-12 months) to ensure your positioning is still sharp and relevant to the market.

---

## Maintaining Your Brand

A personal brand isn't a "set it and forget it" asset. It requires maintenance.

| Frequency | Action | Why |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Quarterly** | **Brand Review** | Re-run the Brand Refresh (Capability E) process. Has your ideal client profile shifted? Have you developed a new, valuable 'Why You'? Is there a new 'Why Now' (new regulation, new technology)? |
| **Monthly** | **Content Performance Check** | Review your LinkedIn analytics. Which of your content pillars (Capability C) is getting the most engagement (likes, comments, shares) from your target audience (Capability B)? Double down on what works. |
| **As Needed** | **Bio Update** | Just closed a major engagement with a great, quantifiable result? Immediately update your Bio Library (Capability D) and LinkedIn profile to include it. Don't wait. |
