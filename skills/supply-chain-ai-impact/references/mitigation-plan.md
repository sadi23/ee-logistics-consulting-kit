# Roadmap for Competitive Advantage
> **Disclaimer:** This document outlines an operational roadmap based on publicly available data and professional experience. It is not financial, investment, or legal advice. All investment figures are estimates and should be validated.

## Overview

This reference provides a structured, month-by-month roadmap for logistics operators to turn the disruption from AI and automation into a competitive advantage.

It is designed to be adapted to the specific client's operation type, risk profile, and capacity to act. It assumes the client wants to be a leader, not a follower.

**Core principles:**

1.  **Quick Wins First:** Month 1 must produce visible progress to build momentum.
2.  **No Big Bets Without Evidence:** Pilots precede rollouts.
3.  **People Drive Success:** The workforce is an asset to be re-skilled, not a cost to be eliminated.
4.  **Self-Funding Where Possible:** Every action should save money, protect revenue, or have a clear ROI case that funds the next step.
5.  **Living Document:** The roadmap is reviewed monthly against market changes.

### Month 2: Knowledge Capture & Workforce Planning

**Primary action:** Document the tacit knowledge in the client's most vulnerable roles.

- Identify the tasks flagged as High Risk in the disruption assessment
- For each High Risk task, document the current process end-to-end: what the person does, what they look at, what decisions they make, what they do when something goes wrong
- Record video or screen captures of expert operators doing the task — this is insurance against losing that knowledge
- Include exception paths: what happens when the normal process breaks? These are the hardest bits to capture and the most valuable
- Start a decision log: "When situation X occurs, what do we do? How do we decide?"

**Secondary action:** Begin workforce communication.

- Share the disruption assessment with leadership first (if the consultant is delivering it, facilitate this conversation)
- Identify which roles are most affected and start a simple skills inventory: what can current staff do, what could they learn, what would they like to learn
- Do NOT announce specific role impacts yet. Instead, communicate the direction: "We see technology changing our industry. We are going to be proactive rather than reactive. Your development matters to us."
- This is the most important communication the client will make. Getting it right builds trust. Getting it wrong creates resistance that can kill the entire plan.

**Milestone:** Knowledge capture document complete for top 5 High Risk tasks. Leadership aligned on the direction and communication approach.

**Owner:** Operations Manager + HR/People lead

**Cost:** Low-to-medium (internal time for knowledge capture; consultant facilitation if needed)

---

### Month 3: Pilot Selection & Supplier Evaluation

**Primary action:** Select the first automation pilot project.

- From the Month 1 audit, pick the single highest-impact, lowest-risk automation opportunity
- Criteria: (1) solves a real pain point the team feels daily, (2) has a clear ROI case, (3) can be piloted without disrupting current operations, (4) has a proven vendor with logistics-specific reference sites
- Write the pilot brief: problem statement, success criteria, timeline, budget, stakeholders, risk assessment

**Secondary action:** Evaluate 2–3 vendors or solutions for the pilot.

- Use the pilot brief to run a structured vendor evaluation
- Require: a site visit to a reference customer, a clear implementation timeline, transparent pricing (no hidden integration or licensing costs), and a warranty period
- Do NOT sign a long-term contract for a first pilot. Demand a 90-day pilot, a month-to-month after that, or a clear exit clause
- For technology procurement: the client should expect to pay for value delivered, not for vendor R&D. If the vendor cannot show working installations in similar operations, walk away.

**Milestone:** Pilot brief approved by leadership. Shortlist of 2–3 vendors with invitations to demo.

**Owner:** Operations Manager + Technology lead (or IT consultant)

**Cost:** Medium (consultant time for pilot selection; vendor demo costs are usually free)

---

### Month 4: Pilot Execution & Learning

**Primary action:** Run the automation pilot.

- Implement the pilot in a controlled environment — one shift, one line, one warehouse, one process
- Measure everything: time per task, error rate, throughput, operator feedback, exception frequency, system uptime
- Run the pilot for at least 2–4 weeks to capture enough data and experience
- Document everything that goes wrong — the edge cases are where the real learning happens
- Get daily operator feedback. They know the process better than anyone. If they hate the pilot, find out why before scaling.

**Secondary action:** Begin reskilling planning.

- Based on the knowledge capture from Month 2 and the pilot experience, start mapping which operators could transition to which new roles
- Options: automation system operator, data analyst, process improvement specialist, customer-facing role, trainer
- Check if the vendor offers training programmes; many do as part of the implementation
- Begin informal career conversations with the most affected team members — ones who have been doing the at-risk task for years

**Milestone:** Pilot completed with documented results (metrics, lessons learned, operator feedback). Preliminary transition map for affected team members.

**Owner:** Operations Manager (pilot execution) + HR/People lead (reskilling)

**Cost:** Medium (pilot software/hardware costs, operator time for training; vendor support costs if applicable)

---

### Month 5: Scale Decision & Roadmap

**Primary action:** Decide whether to scale the pilot.

- Review pilot results against the success criteria set in Month 3
- Three outcomes:
  - **Scale:** Pilot met or exceeded criteria. Develop a full rollout plan.
  - **Pivot:** Pilot showed promise but needs changes. Identify what to fix and run a second iteration.
  - **Kill:** Pilot did not deliver. Document why, capture the learning, and move to the next opportunity on the list.
- For a scale decision: build a 6-month rollout plan with phased implementation, budget, resource requirements, and risk register
- For a kill decision: do not treat it as failure — treating it as learning protects the culture of experimentation the client will need long-term

**Secondary action:** Revisit the disruption assessment.

- The market has moved in 5 months. Run the Phase 1 research again with updated queries
- Have any competitors adopted automation the client should know about?
- Have any technologies matured faster than expected?
- Is the 12-month disruption timeline still accurate, or has it shortened?

**Milestone:** Scale/kill/pivot decision documented. Updated disruption assessment. Full rollout plan (if scaling).

**Owner:** Leadership team (decision) + Operations Manager (rollout plan)

**Cost:** Low (internal time for decision-making; research time for updated assessment)

---

### Month 6: Foundation Laid — Next Phase

**Primary action:** Complete first-phase rollout and establish the automation operating model.

- If scaling: implement Phase 1 of the rollout (e.g. first warehouse, first shift, first process line)
- If pivoting: launch the revised pilot with improvements from the learnings
- If killing: select the next automation opportunity and begin Month 3 again
- Establish governance: who owns automation decisions, how are new opportunities evaluated, what is the budget cycle, who approves pilots vs full rollouts
- Create a simple automation opportunity register — a living document where anyone in the operation can suggest a task for automation

**Secondary action:** Celebrate progress and communicate.

- Share what happened over the 6 months with the whole team — the wins, the learning, and what comes next
- Acknowledge the people who participated in the pilot, the operators who shared their knowledge, the managers who supported the change
- Reinforce the message: "We are building a more resilient operation. Your contribution made this possible."
- Set expectations for the next 6 months: what the client will do, what they will not do, and how the team will be involved

**Milestone:** Phase 1 rollout complete (or revised pilot underway). Automation governance model in place. Automation opportunity register created. Full team communication completed.

**Owner:** Operations Manager + Leadership

**Cost:** Variable (depends on scale decision — from zero for kill/pivot scenarios to implementation costs for scale)

## Budget Planning Reference

Use these ranges for rough-order budget estimates (2025–2026 pricing):

| Automation Type | Approximate Cost (per unit/seat) | ROI Horizon | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| AMR / goods-to-person robot (Locus, GreyOrange, Geek+) | USD 25K–50K per robot (lease 800–1,500/month) | 12–24 months | Scalable in pods; 1 robot replaces 1 picker |
| Autonomous forklift (retrofit) | USD 30K–80K per truck (retrofit) | 18–36 months | Retrofits for existing forklifts cheaper than new |
| Autonomous forklift (new) | USD 80K–150K per truck | 24–36 months | New build with full autonomy stack |
| Automated packaging (right-sized box) | USD 200K–500K per machine | 12–18 months | High throughput justified |
| WMS / TMS upgrade with automation APIs | USD 50K–200K (licensing + implementation) | 12–24 months | Often prerequisite for other automation |
| RPA (robotic process automation) for admin tasks | USD 5K–15K per bot (annual license) | 6–12 months | Good for customs docs, invoicing, data entry |
| Computer vision for inbound/outbound inspection | USD 20K–60K per camera station | 12–18 months | Reduces claims, improves quality |
| Voice-directed picking upgrade | USD 1K–3K per headset | 6–12 months | Low-cost productivity improvement |

**Rule of thumb:** If the automation costs less than 18 months of the manual labour it replaces, the ROI case is strong. If it costs more than 24 months, wait or negotiate.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Picking the wrong first pilot | Excitement about the coolest technology rather than the most impactful | Use the Month 1 audit. Score opportunities on impact AND risk. Pick the highest-impact, lowest-risk |
| Underinvesting in change management | Assuming the technology will sell itself | Budget 20% of the automation cost for change management, training, and communication |
| Going too fast | Pressure from leadership or from a sense of urgency | Month-by-month for a reason. Each phase builds on the last. Skipping Months 1–2 is how pilots fail |
| Going too slow | Paralysis by analysis | If you cannot decide by Month 5, you are overthinking. Make the best call with the data you have |
| Failing to involve operators | Design happens in a conference room | Operators should be in every pilot meeting. They will catch problems the consultants and managers miss |
| Signing a long-term contract | Vendor pressure and fear of missing out | 90-day pilot. Month-to-month after that. Walk away if the vendor insists on a lock-in |
| Not communicating with the team | Fear of causing panic | Silence causes more anxiety than honesty. Communicate the direction early and often |
| Treating automation as a cost play only | Missing the revenue and service quality upside | Frame automation as a competitive advantage, not just a cost-cutting tool |
| Ignoring the data foundation | Trying to automate before the data infrastructure is ready | Fix the data first. Clean, connected data is prerequisite for 80% of automation |
| Doing it alone | Not bringing in expertise for the first automation journey | Use a consultant, a peer network, or a vendor that provides implementation support |
