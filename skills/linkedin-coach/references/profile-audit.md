# LinkedIn Profile Audit — Logistics & Supply Chain

A complete framework for reviewing and optimising a LinkedIn profile for a logistics audience. Work through each section in order.

---

## 1. Headline (the 220-character pitch)

The headline is the most valuable real estate on a LinkedIn profile. It's what appears in search results, connection requests, and the "People Also Viewed" sidebar.

**What we're looking for:**
- Does it signal a specific logistics niche? (Not just "Supply Chain Professional" — that's table stakes.)
- Does it include operational keywords a recruiter or procurement lead would search for?
- Does it differentiate between *role* (what you do) and *value* (who you serve / problems you solve)?

**Common logistics headline patterns:**

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Supply Chain Manager | Supply Chain Manager | Cold Chain Logistics | Pharma Distribution |
| Logistics Expert | Cross-Border Freight Specialist | EU–UK Customs | EORI & CDS Compliance |
| Operations Leader | Head of Logistics Operations | Warehouse Optimisation | 3PL Performance |
| Consultant | Supply Chain Consultant | Freight Procurement | Sourcing–Manufacturing Lead Times |

**Logistics-specific keywords to assess:** freight forwarding, customs compliance, 3PL management, multimodal transport, warehousing, inventory optimisation, last-mile delivery, cold chain, contract logistics, supply chain analytics, procurement, port operations, container shipping, drayage, cross-docking, reverse logistics.

**Check:** If the headline doesn't mention at least one mode of transport, a logistics function, or a supply chain domain, it's too generic.

---

## 2. About Section (the 2,600-character story)

This is where you turn a profile into a narrative. Logistics audiences want to know: *What do you actually move, manage, or improve?*

**Structure to assess:**
1. **Hook** (first 2 lines) — Does it immediately signal the logistics context? Try to avoid "Passionate about supply chain" (everyone says that). Preferred: specific domain + measurable scope. *"I help manufacturers cut landed cost by optimising ocean freight procurement across Asia–Europe lanes."*
2. **The middle** — Is there a clear through-line from past to present? Does it show progression in scope (regional → global, single lane → network, operational → strategic)?
3. **Proof points** — Are there specific metrics, projects, or scale references? *"Managed 50,000 TEUs annually across 12 trade lanes"* tells a story. *"Managed logistics operations"* does not.
4. **The ask / CTA** — Does it tell the reader what to do? (Connect? DM for collaboration? Check out recent writing?)

**Logistics-specific red flags:**
- Entirely generic language that could apply to any industry
- No mention of geography (trade lanes, regions, ports)
- No mention of modes (ocean, air, road, rail, multimodal)
- No indication of scale (volume, value, headcount, locations)
- Jargon that signals *reading about* logistics rather than *doing* logistics (e.g., "leveraging synergies in the value chain")

---

## 3. Experience Section

**What we're looking for in each role:**

- **Role titles** — Do they make sense in a logistics context? (Internal titles like "Logistics Lead" may need context: *Logistics Manager — EMEA Inbound* carries more signal.)
- **Descriptions** — Are they *responsibility-only* or *achievement-driven*? Every bullet should ideally answer: *So what? What changed because of this work?*
- **Metrics** — Percentage improvements, cost reductions, time saved, volume managed are gold. *"Reduced dwell time at Felixstowe by 18% through carrier performance reviews"* — this is the kind of detail a logistics hiring manager stops on.
- **Scope indicators** — Number of warehouses, staff managed, budget, supplier count, trade lanes, SKU volume, countries covered.

**Logistics metrics worth watching for:**
- Cost per container / cost per kg / cost per mile
- On-time delivery / on-time in-full (OTIF)
- Inventory turns / days of inventory outstanding (DIO)
- Freight bill accuracy / audit recovery
- Carrier performance / tender coverage
- Warehouse throughput / pick accuracy
- Customs clearance times / duty recovery
- Carbon footprint / modal shift percentages

**Recommendation check:** Do the bullet points with the strongest metrics sit at the top of each role? Logistics recruiters are busy — front-load the impact.

---

## 4. Featured Section & Keyword Density

The featured section is your portfolio wall. The "About" section tells the story; the "Featured" section proves it.

**What it should contain:**
-   **Your Best PAR Case Study:** A one-page PDF or a link to a blog post detailing a specific client engagement. Lead with the result.
-   **A Strong Point of View:** A link to a high-engagement LinkedIn article you wrote, a podcast you were on, or a presentation you gave.
-   **A "How to Work With Me" Guide:** A simple document outlining your services, your ideal client profile, and how to start a conversation.

**Red Flags:**
-   It's empty. This is wasted real estate.
-   Content is outdated (pinned post from 2 years ago).
-   Content is generic (motivational quotes, reposts of news articles with no commentary).

### Keyword Density Check

LinkedIn's search is not sophisticated, but it relies on keywords. Your profile should be seeded with the terms your ideal client would use to find you.

**How to audit:**
1.  Identify your top 5-10 keywords from your `/personal-brand` positioning statement (e.g., "warehouse control systems," "3PL performance," "customs compliance," "EU logistics").
2.  Read through your **Headline**, **About section**, and the descriptions for your **last two roles**.
3.  Count how many times each keyword appears.
4.  **The Rule:** If your most important keywords don't appear at all in your About section, or only appear once, you have a density problem. You are not signalling your relevance to the search algorithm or the human reader.

**How to fix:**
-   Naturally weave the keywords into your descriptions. Don't "keyword-stuff."
-   **Weak:** "I am an expert in warehouse control systems."
-   **Strong:** "In my last three engagements, I focused on implementing lightweight **warehouse control systems** for mid-market 3PLs, integrating directly with their existing WMS to improve pick accuracy."

## 5. Skills & Endorsements

**Checklist:**
- Are the top three skills genuinely relevant to the target role?
- Is the skills list balanced between *functional* (freight forwarding, customs, warehouse ops) and *strategic* (supply chain design, procurement strategy, cost optimisation)?
- Are endorsements coming from operational peers (real connections who know the work) or mass-endorsed by randoms?

**Logistics-specific skills to look for or suggest:**
- Freight forwarding (Ocean, Air, Road, Rail)
- Customs Compliance & Trade Documentation
- Incoterms & International Trade
- 3PL & 4PL Management
- Warehouse Management Systems (WMS)
- Transport Management Systems (TMS)
- Inventory Planning & Optimisation
- Supply Chain Analytics
- Procurement & Sourcing
- Carrier Relationship Management
- Last-Mile Delivery
- Reverse Logistics
- Lean / Six Sigma in Logistics
- Dangerous Goods (DG) Compliance
- Sustainability / ESG Logistics

---

## 6. Recommendations

Quality over quantity. For logistics profiles:

- 3–5 genuine recommendations from people who've worked alongside you (managers, peers, clients, suppliers) is ideal
- Each should reference a specific logistics outcome or capability
- Generic "great to work with" is near useless. *"She restructured our Rotterdam cross-dock operation and cut mis-sorts by 34% in 90 days"* is gold

**Check:** If the recommender's role is unrelated to logistics, the recommendation may lack credibility for a supply chain audience.

---

## 7. Activity & Engagement

LinkedIn penalises profiles with no recent activity in supply chain search results.

**What to check:**
- Has the profile posted or shared anything in the last 30 days?
- Are comments and engagement on logistics topics or generic industry fluff?
- Does the profile *like* supply chain content (signals algorithm relevance) or scroll past everything?
- Are they following relevant logistics companies, associations, and thought leaders?

**For an inactive profile, the lowest-effort fix:** Engage with 5 supply chain posts per week via meaningful comments. This signals topical relevance without requiring original content creation.

---

## 8. Additional Logistics-Specific Checks

- **Languages:** Logistics is multilingual by nature. If the profile operates in international supply chains but only lists one language, that's a missed signal.
- **Certifications:** CSCP, CLTD, CILT, Lean Six Sigma, Dangerous Goods, Customs Broker Licence — any of these should be prominently visible.
- **Geography:** Is the location listed accurate and relevant? Logistics roles are often tied to specific ports, hubs, or distribution zones.
- **Website / links:** If they run a logistics consultancy, blog, or newsletter, is it linked? A supply chain substack or LinkedIn Newsletter is a strong signal.
