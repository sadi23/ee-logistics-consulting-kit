# Headline Guide — Logistics & Supply Chain

Your LinkedIn headline is the first thing people see in search results, connection requests, and the "People Also Viewed" widget. For logistics professionals, it needs to do three things:

1. **Signal your niche** (what do you actually move, manage, or improve?)
2. **Show operational credibility** (can you speak the language of freight?)
3. **Give a reason to connect** (what's in it for the reader?)

---

## The Formula

Pick a structure that fits your role and target audience.

### Formula A: Role | Domain | Geography
Best for operators and managers. Clearly states what you do, where you do it, and for whom.

```
[Job Title] | [Logistics Domain] | [Geography / Trade Lanes]
```

**Examples:**
- Supply Chain Manager | Cold Chain Logistics | Pharma & Life Sciences | UK–EU
- Freight Operations Lead | Ocean & Air Imports | Asia–Europe Corridors
- Head of Logistics | Cross-Border E-commerce Fulfilment | Europe & US
- Warehouse Operations Manager | 3PL Distribution | Midlands & South East
- Customs Compliance Specialist | CDS & EORI | UK Import–Export

**When to use:** You're a practitioner with defined domain expertise. You want recruiters to find you for a specific type of role.

### Formula B: Problem You Solve | How | For Whom
Best for consultants, founders, and senior leaders. Focuses on *value* rather than *role title*.

```
[What you help clients achieve] | [Your method] | [Target client]
```

**Examples:**
- I help manufacturers cut landed cost by optimising ocean freight procurement | Asia–Europe lanes
- Helping 3PLs improve warehouse throughput through automation and lean operations
- I fix broken supply chains for D2C brands scaling into Europe | Sourcing → Fulfilment
- Reducing customs risk for UK importers | Tariff classification & duty optimisation
- Helping freight forwarders win more business with better RFP response strategies

**When to use:** You sell services, run a consultancy, or want to attract clients rather than employers.

### Formula C: Credential | Focus Area | Differentiator
Best for building personal brand and thought leadership. Establishes authority through credentials and niche focus.

```
[Certification / Title] | [Supply Chain Focus] | [What makes you different]
```

**Examples:**
- CSCP Supply Chain Professional | Inventory Optimisation | Reducing working capital without service impact
- CLTD Certified | Global Logistics & Transportation | Multimodal carrier strategy
- CILT Chartered | Logistics & Transport Operations | Lean transformation in warehousing
- MBA Supply Chain | Procurement & Strategic Sourcing | Supplier relationship management in automotive
- Logistics Consultant | Cross-border Trade & Compliance | China–Europe rail and sea

**When to use:** You have a strong certification or credential that signals competence. Works well for career-changers moving into logistics.

### Formula D: Hybrid (Role + Problem)
Most flexible option. Combines role title with value proposition for maximum searchability.

```
[Role] | [What I do / problem I solve] | [Domain / Geography]
```

**Examples:**
- Supply Chain Consultant | Helping retailers reduce inventory costs while maintaining 98%+ availability
- Freight Forwarding Manager | Ocean procurement & carrier strategy | China–Europe–UK
- Logistics & Operations Director | Warehouse design & 3PL transition | Multi-site distribution networks
- Procurement Lead | Strategic sourcing for packaging & raw materials | FMCG supply chains
- Head of Last-Mile Delivery | Urban logistics optimisation | Parcel carrier management

**When to use:** You want to appear in both role-based and value-based searches. This is the most common pattern for senior professionals.

## Headline Components — What to Include

### Essential (pick at least 2)
- **A logistics domain:** freight forwarding, customs, warehousing, last-mile, cold chain, procurement, supply chain analytics, 3PL, inventory, import/export, multimodal
- **A mode or method:** ocean, air, road, rail, cross-docking, consolidation, direct-to-store, FTL, LTL
- **A geography or trade lane:** Europe, Asia–Pacific, UK–EU, US inbound, China–Europe corridor, Middle East

### Recommended
- **A certification or qualification:** CSCP, CLTD, CILT, Lean Six Sigma, DG safety, Customs Broker
- **A function or discipline:** procurement, operations, planning, compliance, sourcing, logistics engineering
- **An outcome or value:** cost reduction, service improvement, compliance, speed, sustainability, risk management

### Optional
- **A company type or industry:** 3PL, retail, manufacturing, pharma, FMCG, automotive, e-commerce
- **A technology system:** SAP, Oracle, Blue Yonder, Manhattan, JDA, Descartes, WiseTech
- **A specific audience:** "for retailers", "for freight forwarders", "for manufacturers"

## What Not to Do

| Trap | Why | Do This Instead |
|------|-----|-----------------|
| "Supply Chain Professional" | Means nothing. Says what you are, not what you do. | "Supply Chain Manager | Ocean Freight Procurement | Asia–Europe" |
| "Global Logistics Guru" | Sounds like a LinkedIn coach, not a logistics person. | "Logistics Operations | EMEA Warehousing & Distribution" |
| All buzzwords, no substance | "Strategic visionary leveraging synergies in value chain" | "Warehouse & Distribution Manager | Lean & Automation | 3PL Operations" |
| Overly broad geography | "International Logistics" covers too much. | "Cross-Border Logistics | UK–EU Customs & Compliance" |
| Using "Disruptor" in logistics | Nobody in freight forwarding calls themselves this. | "Freight Forwarding | Ocean Consolidation & LCL | UK–Asia" |
| Job title only | "Manager at X Logistics" tells me nothing. | "Logistics Manager | Outbound & Last-Mile | Retail Distribution" |

## Real-World Before / After

### Before (too generic):
> Supply Chain Consultant

**Problems:** No domain, no geography, no value signal. Impossible to differentiate from thousands of others.

**After (clear and specific):**
> Supply Chain Consultant | Helping manufacturers cut landed cost by optimising ocean & air freight procurement | Asia–Europe–UK

**Why it's stronger:** Signals the specific consulting focus, names the mode (ocean & air), identifies the geography, and describes the outcome (cut landed cost).

---

### Before (missing value proposition):
> Logistics Manager at ABC Logistics

**Problems:** Company name does the heavy lifting but the rest is empty. Doesn't tell you what kind of logistics.

**After (complete picture):**
> Logistics Manager | Contract Logistics & Warehousing | 3PL Performance & Transition | Midlands & North West

**Why it's stronger:** Specifies the logistics type, hints at project focus (transition), and narrows geography to a credible operating area.

---

### Before (too many labels, not enough substance):
> Executive Supply Chain Leader | Strategic Visionary | Results-Driven Professional

**Problems:** Reads like a motivational poster. Zero operational signal.

**After (authority + specificity):**
> CILT Chartered | Supply Chain & Logistics Director | Warehouse Design, Automation & Cost Transformation

**Why it's stronger:** Credential first (CILT Chartered), then role, then the actual work they do. A recruiter immediately knows what they'd hire this person for.

## Quick Optimisation Checklist

- [ ] Can someone tell within 3 seconds what you do in logistics?
- [ ] Does it mention at least one mode of transport or logistics function?
- [ ] Is there a geographic or trade-lane reference?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon that a freight forwarder would roll their eyes at?
- [ ] Does the headline pass the "would a supply chain recruiter search for these keywords?" test?
- [ ] Is every word pulling its weight? (220 characters is tight — cut filler words)
- [ ] If you changed the industry to "marketing" would it still make sense? (If yes, it's too generic.)

## A/B Testing Your Headline

You don't have to guess which headline works best. You can test it.

**The Method:**
1.  **Create two strong headline options (A and B)** using different formulas (e.g., Role-focused vs. Problem-focused).
2.  **Run Headline A for 2 weeks.** At the end of the period, record two numbers from your LinkedIn analytics:
    *   **Profile Views:** How many people viewed your profile.
    *   **Search Appearances:** How many times you appeared in search results.
3.  **Switch to Headline B for the next 2 weeks.** At the end of the period, record the same two numbers.
4.  **Compare the results.** Did one headline generate significantly more relevant profile views or search appearances? Be mindful of other factors (like if you published a viral post during one of the periods), but this provides real data on what the market is responding to.

**What to look for:** The goal isn't just more views, it's more *relevant* views. Check the job titles of the people who viewed your profile. If Headline B got fewer views but they were all from your target client profile ("Head of Logistics at a 3PL"), then B is the better headline.

**Option 3:** [Full headline]
