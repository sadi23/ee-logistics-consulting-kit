# Post-Pitch Analysis Reference

## After the Decision

Whether you won or lost, the post-pitch analysis is your most valuable learning tool. Do not skip it. Do not delay it. Do it within 48 hours of hearing the decision while the experience is fresh.

---

## The Three-Gap Framework

Every lost pitch can be traced to one or more of three gaps. Identifying which gap(s) caused the loss tells you exactly what to fix.

### 1. Skill Gap

**Definition:** You lost because your capability, experience, or evidence was genuinely weaker than the winner's.

**How to spot it:**
- The feedback mentions another firm's deeper experience, stronger case studies, or more relevant credentials
- Your case studies were in a different industry, geography, or problem type
- The panel asked questions about areas where you lacked specific examples
- You felt out of your depth during parts of the Q&A

**What to do about it:**
- Identify the specific capability gap. Is it industry knowledge, technical expertise, scale of experience, or methodology?
- Decide whether to close it (training, partnership, hiring) or avoid it (focus on opportunities where you are genuinely strong)
- Build new case studies in the gap area, even if they are smaller or pro bono engagements
- Consider partnering with another consultant who fills the gap for future bids

### 2. Signal Gap

**Definition:** You had the capability but failed to communicate it convincingly. The panel did not see your full strength.

**How to spot it:**
- The feedback says "we did not see enough evidence" but you actually have relevant experience
- The panel asked questions about things you covered in your proposal -- meaning your written or verbal message did not land
- Your case studies were in the proposal but you did not reference them in the pitch
- A competitor with similar capability won because they told a better story

**What to do about it:**
- Rewrite your executive summary and pitch opening. Lead with your strongest, most relevant result.
- Train yourself to answer every question by connecting it to a specific case study or data point within 10 seconds
- Record your pitch practice and check: are you spending too much time on background and not enough on proof?
- Get a peer to review your proposal and pitch deck. Ask them: "What is my strongest capability? What is my second strongest?" If they cannot answer, the signal is weak.
- Simplify. Fewer messages, repeated more clearly, with better evidence.

### 3. Fit Gap

**Definition:** You lost for reasons beyond your control or unrelated to capability -- budget mismatch, incumbent lock-in, internal politics, personal chemistry, or timing.

**How to spot it:**
- The feedback is vague or complimentary but the decision went elsewhere
- You were eliminated before the pitch stage despite strong credentials
- The budget was clearly below your typical engagement size
- The incumbent won despite apparently weaker capability
- You felt the chemistry was off during the pitch -- you never connected with the panel
- The panel chose a lower-cost option when you offered higher value

**What to do about it:**
- Improve your bid/no-bid filter. If you cannot compete on price, do not bid on price-driven procurements.
- Invest in relationship building before the RFP is issued. Fit gaps often reflect a lack of early engagement.
- Ask directly about budget range before investing in a full proposal.
- Be more selective. A fit gap loss is not a failure -- it is a waste of time only if you do not learn from it.
- For future opportunities with this client, start building the relationship now, before the next RFP.

## Win/Loss Analysis Template

Use this template within 48 hours of the decision.

### If You Won

**Congratulations. Now analyse why.**

| Question | Your Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| What was the single most important factor in winning? | |
| Which of our win themes resonated most? | |
| Which part of the pitch made the difference? (proposal, presentation, Q&A, reference, price) | |
| What almost lost us the deal? | |
| What did the winner (us) do better than the runner-up? | |
| What was unexpected during the process? | |
| What would we do differently next time? | |
| What relationships should we maintain and how? | |

**Action Items:**
1. Write down the winning formula -- what specific combination of capability, messaging, relationship, and timing worked.
2. Identify the near-loss factors -- fix those before the next pitch.
3. Send thank-you notes to everyone involved. Nurture the relationship for the next opportunity.

### If You Lost

**Disappointment is allowed. Then analyse.**

| Question | Your Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| Who won? What do we know about them? | |
| What was the stated reason for the decision? | |
| What do we think the real reason was? (It is often different from the stated reason.) | |
| Which gap(s) were primary: Skill, Signal, Fit? | |
| What specific feedback did we get? | |
| Where in the process did we lose ground? (RFP response, pitch, Q&A, pricing, references) | |
| What did the winner do that we did not? | |
| What did we do that the winner did not? (Was it valuable or wasted?) | |
| Could we have won? Or was it unwinnable from the start? | |
| Should we have bid at all? | |
| What would we change for a similar opportunity in the future? | |

**Action Items:**
1. Identify the primary gap (Skill, Signal, or Fit) -- and address it with a specific action.
2. If the gap is fixable, build a plan. If not, adjust your bid/no-bid criteria.
3. Send a gracious follow-up. The procurement lead will remember you handled rejection well. That builds long-term relationships.
4. Book the next RFP or pitch opportunity within 30 days. Momentum is the best cure for rejection.

### If You Never Got a Decision (Ghosted)

**This happens more than it should. It is data about the client's process maturity, not about you.**

| Question | Your Answer |
|---|---|
| When was our last communication? | |
| Have we followed up appropriately? (1-week check-in, 2-week final follow-up) | |
| What does our pipeline tracker say? Is this past the expected close date? | |
| Do we have a non-procurement relationship we can leverage for an informal update? | |
| If the opportunity is truly dead, what did we learn about this client's procurement process? (e.g., "They are chronically disorganized," "They use RFPs for price discovery only") | |
| Should we adjust our bid/no-bid criteria for this client in the future? | |

**Action Items:**
1.  Send one final, professional follow-up. Assume the best (the process is slow, not personal).
2.  If no response within 10 working days, close the opportunity in your pipeline tracker. Time-box your effort.
3.  Log the client and procurement process quirks in your CRM for future reference.
4.  Reflect on early signals. Were there warning signs of a disorganized or uncommunicative buyer? Add these to your bid/no-bid red flags.

## The Feedback Loop: Connecting Analysis to Action

**This is the most important step.** Your analysis is useless if it doesn't inform your next move. Every win, loss, or ghosting must feed back into your other skills.

| Finding From Analysis | Action Required | Target Skill to Update |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Loss on price (Fit Gap)** | Your value proposition isn't strong enough to justify the premium. | `/personal-brand` (revisit "Why You") |
| **Loss on experience (Skill Gap)** | Your case studies are not relevant enough. | `/pitch-master` (build a new PAR case study) |
| **Loss on messaging (Signal Gap)** | You have the skill, but it didn't land. | `/pitch-master` (run more mock pitches) and `/linkedin-coach` (clarify your headline and "About" section) |
| **Incumbent won (Fit Gap)** | You need to build relationships earlier. | `/engagement-navigator` (refine lead gen and networking strategy) |
| **Ghosted by a certain client type** | This client profile is a time-waster. | `/engagement-navigator` (update your bid/no-bid filter to screen them out earlier) |
| **Win based on a specific win theme** | This message resonates strongly. | `/personal-brand` and `/linkedin-coach` (double down on this theme in your content and positioning) |

**Mandatory Action:** After completing a Win/Loss Analysis, you *must* open the relevant skill and make one specific update based on your findings. This closes the loop and ensures you don't repeat the same mistakes.

## Pattern Recognition Over Time

A single loss is a data point. Five losses with the same gap is a trend. Track your pitches over time to spot patterns.

### What to Look For

**Skill Gap Patterns:**
- Repeated losses in a specific industry? Build industry knowledge or stop bidding there.
- Repeated losses on technology engagements? Partner with a tech specialist.
- Repeated losses on cost reduction bids? Strengthen your cost data and benchmarking.

**Signal Gap Patterns:**
- Repeated losses despite relevant experience? Your messaging needs work. Get a copywriter or presentation coach.
- Repeated feedback that your proposal was "thorough but not compelling"? You are writing for compliance, not persuasion.
- Repeated losses after strong proposals but weak pitches? Focus on pitch delivery practice.

**Fit Gap Patterns:**
- Repeated losses to incumbents? Invest more in pre-RFP relationship building.
- Repeated losses on price? Adjust your bid/no-bid filter or develop a lower-cost service model.
- Repeated losses where the client went with a less qualified firm? You may be chasing the wrong type of client.
- Repeated ghosting on specific sectors or geographies? Consider whether those buyers are worth pursuing.

## Recovery After Rejection

### Immediate (Day 1-3)

1. **Feel it.** You invested time, energy, and hope. Acknowledging the disappointment is healthier than pretending it does not matter.
2. **Analyse it.** Use the win/loss template above. Do not let the emotional response prevent the learning.
3. **Respond graciously.** Send a short, professional email. Thank them for the opportunity. Ask if they are willing to share feedback. Do not argue.

   *Example:*
   > Dear [Name],
   >
   > Thank you for the opportunity to participate in this process. We appreciate the time your team invested.
   >
   > We would welcome any feedback that might help us improve. If you are able to share what set the successful bid apart, we would be grateful.
   >
   > We wish you and [Client] every success with the chosen partner.
   >
   > Best regards,
   > [Your Name]

4. **Book the next one.** The best antidote to a lost pitch is a new opportunity. Find it and start preparing within 30 days.

### Medium Term (Week 2-4)

1. **Revisit your bid/no-bid criteria.** Did you chase an unwinnable opportunity? Adjust the filter.
2. **Fill the gap.** Based on the analysis, take one concrete action to close the primary gap.
3. **Strengthen your case study bank.** If the gap was evidence, build a new case study.
4. **Update your pitch deck and proposal template.** Incorporate lessons learned.

### Long Term (Month 1-6)

1. **Track your win rate.** If it is below 30%, something systematic needs to change.
2. **Build relationships before RFPs.** The best way to avoid fit gaps is to be known before the procurement starts.
3. **Develop a referral network.** Warm introductions convert at a much higher rate than cold RFPs.
4. **Consider specialisation.** The more specific your niche, the fewer competitors you face and the higher your win rate.

## Common Rejection Reasons and What They Really Mean

| Stated Reason | Likely Real Reason | What to Address |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| "Price was higher" | They did not see enough value to justify the premium | Signal Gap -- strengthen value articulation |
| "Another firm had more relevant experience" | Your case studies were not convincing or not close enough | Skill Gap or Signal Gap -- build better evidence or communicate it better |
| "We decided to go with the incumbent" | You did not build the relationship early enough | Fit Gap -- invest in pre-RFP engagement |
| "We are postponing the project" | Budget cut, political change, or they chose someone else | Could be genuine delay or polite rejection -- follow up in 3-6 months |
| "You were a very strong contender" | You lost. This is a polite way of saying it was close. | Ask for specifics. The real reason is in the follow-up conversation. |
| "We decided to keep it in-house" | They did not trust an external provider, or internal politics blocked it | Fit Gap -- relationship and trust building needed before the next opportunity |
| No feedback given | Either the decision was political, or they do not want to invest time in a rejected bidder | Accept it and move on. Not every loss deserves analysis. |
