# Mock Pitch Reference

## How to Run a Mock Pitch Session

A mock pitch is a full-dress rehearsal of your pitch deck, Q&A responses, and personal presence. It is not a casual run-through -- it should be as close to the real thing as possible.

### Setup

1. **Set the scene.** Choose a real or realistic RFP scenario. The facilitator plays the procurement panel (see personas below). You present as if it is the real pitch.
2. **Timebox it.** A typical pitch slot is 45-90 minutes. Allocate:
   - 15-20 minutes for your presentation
   - 25-40 minutes for Q&A
   - 10 minutes for feedback
3. **Record it.** Video if possible, audio at minimum. You cannot assess your own body language and tone in real time.
4. **No breaks, no resets.** If you stumble, recover as you would in the real room. The point is to practice recovery.
5. **Invite a devil's advocate.** If you have a trusted colleague or mentor, have them play the toughest panel member. If not, the assistant will do it.

### Session Flow

| Phase | Duration | What Happens |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Opening | 2 min | Panel lead sets context, introductions, format |
| Your Pitch | 15-20 min | You present. No interruptions. Panel takes notes. |
| Panel Q&A | 25-40 min | Panel asks live questions. You answer in real time. |
| Your Close | 2-3 min | Summary of why you, what next, confidence statement |
| Feedback | 10 min | Panel gives candid feedback on content and delivery |

### Rules for the Panel (Facilitator)

- Ask real questions you would ask in a real procurement. No softball questions.
- Push back on vague answers. Say "That sounds generic. Can you be more specific?"
- Interrupt if the answer goes too long or goes off-track. Real panels do this.
- Use silence. After the candidate answers, wait 5-7 seconds before asking the next question. Let them feel the pressure to fill the gap.
- Vary your tone: friendly one question, sceptical the next, indifferent after that.
- At the end, give honest, specific feedback. Say what worked, what did not, and what must change before the real pitch.

## Procurement Panel Personas

The assistant will adopt one or more of these personas during the mock session. Each persona has a distinct line of questioning and evaluation criteria.

### Persona 1: Sarah Chen -- Procurement Lead

**Role:** Runs the procurement process. Has done 40+ logistics RFPs. She has seen every trick, every buzzword, every overpromise.

**What she cares about (in order):**
1. Compliance -- did you follow the instructions? Did you answer the question asked?
2. Risk -- what happens if you do not deliver? What recourse does the client have?
3. Value -- not lowest price, but best value. Can you prove the ROI?
4. References -- will your past clients say good things? Has she already called them?
5. Commercial model -- is it transparent? Are there hidden fees?

**Her tells:**
- Takes detailed notes throughout. If she stops writing, you have lost her.
- Asks the same question three different ways to see if the answer changes.
- Prefers silence to filling the gap. She wants to see how you handle discomfort.

**Typical questions from Sarah:**
- "Walk me through your fee structure. What is included? What is not?"
- "Your case study mentions a 22% cost reduction. How do I know that is repeatable here?"
- "Tell me about a time you missed a target. What happened and what did you learn?"
- "Who specifically would be on my account day-to-day? I want names and CVs."
- "If we sign today, what does the first 30 days look like?"
- "What is the one thing you are not telling us because you think we do not want to hear it?"

### Persona 2: Marcus Okonkwo -- Operations Director

**Role:** Lives with the problem every day. He does not care about your methodology slides. He cares whether your plan works in his network, with his people, on his watch.

**What he cares about (in order):**
1. Feasibility -- can you actually do what you are promising in this specific environment?
2. Practicality -- what does Monday morning look like? How do you transition without disrupting operations?
3. People -- how will you handle his warehouse managers, transport planners, and customer service team?
4. Track record -- have you solved his kind of problem before? Not vaguely similar -- actually the same.
5. Communication -- how often will he hear from you? In what format? When things go wrong, how do you escalate?

**His tells:**
- Leans forward when you talk about operational specifics. Leans back when you get theoretical.
- Asks about exceptions, edge cases, and failure modes more than the happy path.
- Gets visibly frustrated with jargon or consultants who cannot explain how something actually works.

**Typical questions from Marcus:**
- "Your approach sounds good on paper. But our network is different. Walk me through how this would work at our Nottingham site specifically."
- "What happens in week one? What do you need from my team? What do I need to stop doing?"
- "We tried a similar initiative three years ago. It failed. Tell me why you would not fail the same way."
- "How do you handle a site manager who does not want to change?"
- "My best transport planner is retiring in six months. What is your handover plan?"
- "What is the hardest operational problem you have solved in the last 12 months? Tell me exactly how you solved it."
- "Your timeline shows 18 months. What if I need results in 12?"

### Persona 3: Priya Sharma -- Finance Director / Commercial Lead

**Role:** Protects the budget. She is not anti-spend -- she is anti-waste. She needs to justify this investment to the board.

**What she cares about (in order):**
1. TCO (Total Cost of Ownership) -- what is the full cost over the contract term, not just year one?
2. ROI -- what return does the business get? How is it measured? When does it arrive?
3. Commercial model -- fixed price? T&M? Outcome-based? Gain share? What are the risks?
4. Hidden costs -- transition costs, change request margins, termination penalties, inflation clauses
5. Financial stability -- is your business healthy? Will you be here in three years?

**Her tells:**
- Brings a calculator. Checks your numbers as you speak.
- Asks about assumptions behind every financial claim.
- If she stops asking questions, she has decided. You either made the case or you did not.

**Typical questions from Priya:**
- "You claim 22% cost reduction. Show me the assumptions behind that number."
- "What is the total investment required in year one before the savings start?"
- "How do you handle scope creep? What is your change request process and pricing?"
- "What happens if we want to terminate after 12 months? What are the penalties?"
- "Can you share your company accounts or a Dunn & Bradstreet report?"
- "How do you benchmark your fees against the market? What makes your pricing competitive?"
- "Your commercial model has a monthly retainer plus a success fee. What if the savings do not materialise?"

### Persona 4: David Thompson -- End User / Stakeholder

**Role:** Will have to live with the outcome of this decision. His team works in the system you design. He is often overlooked in the pitch process.

**What he cares about (in order):**
1. Day-to-day experience -- will this make my job easier or harder?
2. Training and support -- how will you help my team learn the new way?
3. Communication -- will I know what is happening? Will I be consulted?
4. Stability -- will operations be disrupted during the transition?
5. Respect -- will you listen to me? My team has been running this network for years.

**His tells:**
- Asks questions about things that annoy his team daily.
- Looks for signs that the consultant respects operational knowledge vs bringing in a pre-baked solution.
- Will give you a chance -- but if you dismiss his experience, you lose him permanently.

**Typical questions from David:**
- "My team has been doing this job for an average of 12 years. How will you earn their trust?"
- "What does training actually look like? Is it a PDF manual or hands-on support?"
- "How do you handle the go-live weekend? Will there be someone on-site?"
- "What happens when something breaks at 10pm on a Friday? Who do we call and how fast do they respond?"
- "I have seen consultants come and go. What makes you different?"

### Persona 5: The Distracted Executive (Bonus Round)

**Role:** The ultimate decision-maker, often the budget holder. They have been pulled into this pitch between three other meetings. You have 15 minutes of their partial attention.

**What they care about:**
1.  The one-sentence summary.
2.  The financial outcome (cost saved or revenue enabled).
3.  The risk of this project failing and making them look bad.
4.  Whether you sound credible enough to delegate this to.

**Their tells:**
-   Checks their phone/watch constantly.
-   Will interrupt you mid-sentence with a blunt question like "What's the point?" or "How much does it cost?".
-   May have to leave early.

**How to handle them:**
-   Lead with the outcome in the first 30 seconds.
-   Use the elevator pitch version of your case study.
-   Answer questions directly and then stop talking.
-   Have a one-page summary slide ready to go.

### Persona 6: The Skeptical Incumbent (Hard Mode)

**Role:** An internal manager or a rival consultant who has a long-standing relationship with the client. They are on the panel to defend their turf and make you look bad.

**What they care about:**
1.  Protecting their position.
2.  Finding flaws in your methodology.
3.  Highlighting risks you haven't mentioned.
4.  Proving you don't understand the client's "unique" culture.

**Their tells:**
-   Asks highly detailed, "gotcha" questions designed to trip you up.
-   Will say things like, "That sounds good in theory, but here's why it won't work here..."
-   Will often reference historical context you couldn't possibly know.

**How to handle them:**
-   **Acknowledge their expertise:** "That's a great point, and you know this operation better than I do. Here's how we've handled a similar challenge at..."
-   **Don't get defensive:** Stay calm and stick to your data.
-   **Reframe their challenges as opportunities:** "You're right, that cultural resistance is a major risk. That's why our change management plan is so critical..."

## Likely Procurement Panel Questions (15-20)

These are the questions that come up in almost every logistics consulting pitch. Practise your answers until they sound natural, not rehearsed.

### Part 1: Credential and Experience Questions

1. "Tell us about yourself and why you are the right person for this engagement."
2. "What relevant experience do you have with our industry / geography / problem type?"
3. "Have you worked with companies of our size and complexity before?"
4. "Can you share a case study that is similar to what we need? What was the result?"
5. "Who else is on your team? What are their specific qualifications?"

### Part 2: Approach and Methodology Questions

6. "Walk us through how you would approach this engagement from day one."
7. "What methodology or framework do you use? Why that one?"
8. "How do you handle resistance to change within the client organisation?"
9. "What data do you need from us, and when do you need it?"
10. "How do you ensure knowledge transfer so we are not dependent on you forever?"

### Part 3: Commercial and Risk Questions

11. "How do you structure your fees? What is included and what is chargeable extra?"
12. "What are the biggest risks in this engagement and how do you mitigate them?"
13. "What happens if you do not deliver the results you are promising?"
14. "How do you handle scope creep?"
15. "Why should we hire you instead of [competitor / Big 4 firm / in-house team]?"

### Part 4: Tough Questions (these separate the strong from the weak)

16. "Tell me about a project that went wrong. What happened and what did you learn?"
17. "Your case study results look impressive. How do I know they are repeatable here?"
18. "What is the one thing you would change about your approach if you could do it again?"
19. "We have had bad experiences with consultants before. Why would you be different?"
20. "If we do not hire you, what advice would you give us for free?"

## Scoring Rubric for Mock Pitches

Use this to score your performance in a mock session. Aim for 4+ in every category.

| Category | 1 (Poor) | 2 (Weak) | 3 (Adequate) | 4 (Good) | 5 (Excellent) |
|----------|----------|----------|--------------|----------|----------------|
| **Clarity** | Rambling, unfocused | Gets there eventually | Clear structure | Crisp and logical | Memorable, quotable |
| **Specificity** | Vague, generic | Some specifics | Relevant examples | Concrete and detailed | You can visualise it |
| **Credibility** | No evidence | Weak evidence | Relevant case studies | Strong, quantified proof | You are the obvious choice |
| **Listening** | Ignores the question | Answers something else | Answers the question | Addresses the real concern | Anticipates unasked questions |
| **Presence** | Nervous, defensive | Stiff, scripted | Professional | Confident and calm | Commanding, likeable |
| **Adaptability** | Flustered by tough questions | Recovers slowly | Answers well | Handles curveballs | Uses tough questions as strength |
| **Closing** | Weak or no close | Generic close | Summarises well | Memorable close | Leaves them wanting to hire you |

## Post-Mock Debrief

After each mock session, answer these questions honestly:

1. **What went well?** (Which answers landed? Which parts felt natural?)
2. **What felt shaky?** (Where did you hesitate, ramble, or dodge?)
3. **Which question caught you off guard?** (Did you have a good answer? If not, prepare one.)
4. **What did the panel push back on?** (Is this a real weakness or just a communication gap?)
5. **What will you change before the real pitch?** (One specific action, not a general resolution.)
